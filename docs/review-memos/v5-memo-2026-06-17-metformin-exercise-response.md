# Alpha memo: metformin, exercise response, and mitochondrial adaptation

## Core signal
The MASTERS trial directly contradicts its own preregistered hypothesis. Despite launching a randomized controlled trial (Receipt 2) on the premise that metformin's anti-inflammatory profile would *augment* the response to strength training in adults aged 65+, the published results (Receipt 1) show placebo gained **more** lean body mass (p = .003), thigh muscle mass (p < .001), thigh muscle area (p = .005), and thigh muscle density (p = .020). Strength gains also trended *negative* on metformin without reaching significance. The mechanistic fingerprint is a directional split: metformin **increased** AMPK signaling while **blunting** the mTORC1 response to PRT, and it prevented the placebo group's drop in type I fiber percentage (p = .007). The trial is a registered clinicaltrials.gov / multicenter RCT — a high-credibility negative result sitting on top of a positive-sounding trial name ("MASTERS — Metformin to **Augment** Strength Training Effective Response in Seniors").

## The 2+2=5 angle
A protocol titled "Metformin to **Augment** Strength Training" (Receipt 2) registering on clinicaltrials.gov is, by design, a positive-valence framing exercise. The same trial's published outcome (Receipt 1) is a **blunting** signal. Reading them together exposes a boundary condition hidden inside the MASTERS design: the inflammation-suppression hypothesis assumes inflammatory signaling is the bottleneck for hypertrophy in older adults, but the receipt-level data implies that whatever AMPK activation metformin adds is *subtracting* from the mTORC1-driven anabolism that PRT actually requires. Two well-formed inputs (a clean RCT, a clean mechanism) combine to a non-obvious fifth conclusion: in 65+ adults, anti-inflammatory pharmacology can be **counterproductive** when stacked on top of a stimulus whose efficacy depends on transient inflammatory signaling.

## Why this could matter
Most metformin-exercise literature is anchored in cardiometabolic or aerobic endpoints. The MASTERS receipt is one of the few prospective, blinded, registered trials isolating the hypertrophy axis in the exact population most likely to be co-prescribed metformin (older adults). The asymmetry between the preregistered "augment" language and the "blunts" finding (Receipts 1 & 2) is the kind of receipt-anchored contradiction that is easy to miss when each paper is read in isolation.

## What would break the idea
- Dose: 1,700 mg/day is fixed in the trial; lower doses may not engage the AMPK/mTORC1 split seen in Receipt 1.
- Population: receipt is 65+ healthy adults; effect direction in younger or insulin-resistant cohorts is not established by these receipts.
- Training stimulus: 14 weeks of supervised PRT only; aerobic or concurrent training responses are outside the supplied evidence.
- Fiber-type readouts (p = .007) suggest a remodeling signal that the receipts do not fully resolve — flagged as a hypothesis, not a finding.

## Receipts
- 10.1111/acel.13039 — MASTERS trial, Aging Cell 2019: metformin blunts hypertrophy, ↑AMPK, ↓mTORC1 trend.
- 10.1186/s13063-017-1932-5 — MASTERS protocol, Trials 2017: preregistered "augment" hypothesis and design.

## Safety note
Memo is a research synthesis, not clinical advice. Do not extrapolate to prescribing decisions; consult a clinician for metformin use.
