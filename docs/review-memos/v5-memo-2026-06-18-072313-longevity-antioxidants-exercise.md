# Alpha memo: antioxidant stacks may not travel cleanly across endurance and strength adaptation

## Core signal
Two human trials, both giving vitamin C + E versus placebo around supervised training, point in opposite directions on the same supplement stack:

- **Endurance, 12 weeks (Receipt 10.1249/mss.0b013e3181cd76be, 2010).** In moderately trained young men, vitamin C + E versus placebo "did not" blunt gains in VO₂max, maximal power, lactate threshold workload, muscle glycogen, citrate synthase, or β-HAD activity. The authors explicitly frame the commercial antioxidant-in-sports market as the backdrop.
- **Strength, 10 weeks (Receipt 10.1113/jphysiol.2014.279950, 2014).** In recreationally strength-trained men and women, high-dose vitamin C + E reduced p70S6K, p38, and ERK1/2 phosphorylation after a session. Hypertrophy was not significantly blunted, but some strength measures rose less than in placebo.

The bridge is non-obvious because both studies use the same ingredients, same placebo design, and same population segment (healthy young individuals) — only the training mode differs.

## The 2+2=5 angle
A simple read of the longevity stack — "vitamins, exercise, adaptation" — assumes the supplement either helps or hurts. The receipts together imply the answer is mode-dependent: the same antioxidant regimen that did not blunt the measured endurance outputs in the 2010 receipt can shift acute strength-training protein signaling in a negative direction. The hidden boundary condition is **training modality (endurance vs strength)**, not dose, population, or duration (those are roughly matched between trials). Longevity protocols that lump "exercise" together may mis-attribute adaptation outcomes to supplements that are actually modality-specific.

## Why this could matter
For longevity interventions bundling antioxidant stacks with mixed training, the receipts suggest:
- In one 12-week endurance-training trial in moderately trained young men, vitamin C + E did not blunt the measured endurance-performance and enzyme adaptations.
- It may blunt strength adaptation via redox-sensitive MAPK/mTOR signaling, even if hypertrophy looks intact in the same trial.
- The commercial "need for antioxidant supplementation" claim (Receipt 10.1249/mss.0b013e3181cd76be) is uncorroborated in both modalities, and partially contradicted in strength.

## What would break the idea
- A replication of the strength trial showing no phosphorylation or strength decrement — then the signaling hit is noise.
- A trial in older or clinical populations, where antioxidant effects could diverge from the young healthy cohort both studies use.
- A direct head-to-head endurance-vs-strength design with shared lab, dose, and subjects.

## Receipts
- 10.1249/mss.0b013e3181cd76be — endurance trial, 2010, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*.
- 10.1113/jphysiol.2014.279950 — strength trial, 2014, *The Journal of Physiology*.

## Safety note
Both receipts describe vitamin C + E in research settings around supervised training in healthy young individuals. This memo is not clinical advice. Any supplementation decision belongs to a qualified clinician, and the "high dosages" flagged in 10.1113/jphysiol.2014.279950 should not be self-administered on the basis of this memo.
