# Alpha memo: AI coding assistants, productivity, and review quality

## Core signal
A 2025 interview study of 20 software engineers (DOI: 10.1109/chase66643.2025.00016) reports that Large Language Model (LLM)-assisted code reviews reduce the need for emotional regulation and coping mechanisms relative to human peer review, but cognitive load can rise because LLM feedback is excessively detailed and adoption is constrained by trust and missing context. The same study frames code review as a cognitive, emotional, and behavioral process disrupted by the introduction of AI into collaborative workflows.

A separate 2018 orthopaedic compendium foreword (DOI: 10.1097/bot.0000000000001063) documents a long-running cooperative effort between the AO Foundation and the Orthopaedic Trauma Association (OTA) to standardize fracture and dislocation classification, with explicit "collaborative support" and a shared-copyright arrangement intended to keep reproduction and promulgation "unencumbered."

## The 2+2=5 angle
Both receipts treat a *collaborative workflow* as the unit of value, not the individual contributor. The orthopaedic foreword treats classification standardization as a collaboration-maintenance problem (joint copyright, joint funding, joint committee). The SE interview study treats code review as a collaboration-disruption problem when AI is introduced. The non-obvious bridge: a 20-person interview finding about emotional regulation in LLM code review may behave like a two-organization classification-standards question — institutional scaffolding (governance, shared IP, terminology control) is what allows a community to absorb a new "reviewer" without losing trust or context. The receipts do not cite each other; this is a hypothesis about structural analogy, not a tested result.

## Why this could matter
The interview study (n=20, qualitative, unknown venue) suggests that productivity-style metrics for AI coding assistants miss the friction surface: trust, context loss, and emotional load. If the orthopaedic model generalizes, the binding constraint is not model quality but collaborative infrastructure (shared vocabularies, IP/governance, review norms) — a channel/product implication limited to SE code-review workflows as studied in that one 2025 paper.

## What would break the idea
- The SE study is n=20 interviews at one venue; quantitative replication could overturn the cognitive-load and trust findings.
- The orthopaedic analogy is cross-domain and not invoked in either receipt; mismatch in regulation, liability, and reviewer accountability could invalidate the comparison.
- If a separate study showed LLM feedback is calibrated to context, the "excessive details" finding would collapse.
